Here's a brief description of some of the methods used to derive polyhedra appearing on this list:

Crimping

Crimping is a term I coined to describe the process of "pinching off" the corners of a polyhedron. Functionally, this can be described as cutting the corners with the surface of a pyramid with its edges directly between those of the corner. In most cases, these pyramids are based halfway along the edges belonging to the corner, with one exception (the alternating crimped cube) where it is instead based at the far ends of the edges.

a crimped tetrahedron

Double Crimping

This is nearly the same as crimping, but resulting in twice as many new "pinched" faces. Constructed by pyramids with base polygons that have twice as many sides as with crimping. This process shares the exact same quirks about where such pyramids are based.

a tetrahedron

a double crimped tetrahedron

Stellation

This process is not the same as the more documented geometric process that shares its name. It could be thought of as more generalized, with "true" stellation being a specific case. Concerning monodihedral polyhedra, stellation basically amounts to placing a pyramid onto a preexisting face of a polyhedron. If done to specific faces, this can result in a topology capable of being monodihedral.

a cuboctahedron

a fully stellated cuboctahedron

Truncation

Like stellation, this is a more general process than the preexisting one in geometry by the same name. In the context of monodihedral polyhedra, truncation is essentially "chopping off" specific corners of a polyhedron to create a new topology that can then be adjusted to be monodihedral.

a double crimped tetrahedron

a truncated double crimped tetrahedron

"Propellor" Operation

This process, not discovered by me, involves "pulling out" the faces of a platonic solid and rotating them all by the same amount before connecting all the old and new vertices together. There are many versions of this operation, but one of them specifically creates monodihedral polyhedra. This class of solids is unique for being chiral (they essentially have a "handedness" as they don't have any mirror symmetry), a trait shared only by the pentagonal catalan solids among monodihedral polyhedra.
